Regression Analysis / Atlanta

The Shape of a Commute, In Atlanta

I took the same analysis I worked on with a team for I-94 in Minneapolis and ran it on 35 days of hourly data from the Downtown Connector. The story holds, with Atlanta flourishes. Here is how it looked, one model at a time.

Quan Duong · Independent follow-up to the ISyE 6414 group project

The I-85 sensor just north of the I-75/85 split is one of the busiest traffic counters in the Atlanta region. Over 35 days in late spring 2025, from April 19 to May 23, it recorded 840 hourly observations of southbound traffic.

Would the same modeling approach that worked on Minneapolis I-94 work here? And would the answer be the same?

Short version: yes on both counts. The story is cleaner than I expected.

The raw data

Every dot below is a single hour of southbound volume. No cleaning was needed for duplicates in this window, though a longer pull from the Georgia Department of Transportation portal would almost certainly have some.

The weekly pulse

Zoom into a single week and the familiar rhythm appears. Sharp weekday spikes, softer weekend humps. The pattern is unmistakable. Weekends are shaded in orange.

The average day

Fold all 35 days onto a single 24 hour axis and the bimodal curve is just as sharp as its Minneapolis counterpart. A morning peak around 7 AM and a bigger evening peak around 5 PM. The Atlanta curve has a taller afternoon shoulder, which suggests more midday commercial traffic than Minneapolis experiences.

This is the single shape that drives the entire regression. A straight-line model cannot fit a two-peaked curve, so we will need hour as a factor.

Weekday vs weekend

Split by weekday and weekend. Weekday keeps the sharp rush peaks. Weekend flattens, with a single gentle hill near midday that rises earlier and falls later than Minneapolis weekends.

The interaction term hour_f × weekend_indicator will capture this. In Minneapolis it lifted the model from 0.85 to 0.95. In Atlanta, the same move takes us from 0.87 to 0.97.

How do I measure a good model?

Adjusted R-squared is the main scorecard: what fraction of the variation in traffic does the model actually explain? A score of 0.22 means the model misses 78 percent of the reasons traffic goes up and down. A score of 0.98 means it captures nearly everything. Adjusted R-squared also penalizes dumping unnecessary variables into the model, which keeps the scorecard honest.

0.23 → 0.98 Adjusted R²
Model 1 to Model 5

Five models, one scorecard

Each model adds exactly one idea to the previous one. That discipline makes the comparison interpretable.

Model 1 treats hour as a plain number. It fits a straight line and misses both rush peaks. Adjusted R² = 0.23.

Model 2 treats hour as a factor with 24 separate effects. The curve now traces rush hours. 0.87.

Model 3 adds the hour_f × weekend_indicator interaction. 0.97.

Model 4 takes the log of traffic volume as a partial fix for residual fan-out at high volumes. 0.97.

Model 5 adds the previous hour's volume as a predictor, which largely resolves autocorrelation. 0.98.

Residual diagnostics

A good model's residuals should look like random noise around zero. A pattern in the residuals is the model failing to explain something. Here are Model 2's residuals on the left and Model 3's on the right.

The one hour lag

Model 3 still had autocorrelated residuals. The Durbin-Watson statistic was 0.69, well below the clean value of 2.0. Adding the lag of traffic volume from one hour earlier pushes it to 1.99, which is essentially ideal. The scatter below shows why the lag predictor works so well.

Atlanta vs Minneapolis, side by side

The two regressions tell the same story with different coefficients.

The conclusion I drew from the team project holds here too. Time of day and day of week are the dominant predictors of hourly traffic. Weather, which I could not pull for Atlanta without a separate NOAA merge, is likely marginal by comparison. The practical implication is the same: if you want to smooth congestion, shift when people drive, not how they respond to rain.
