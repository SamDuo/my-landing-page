A data journey through 4,411 hours

The Shape of a Commute

Every dot above is one hour of traffic on I-94 near Minneapolis. Keep scrolling. The chart will zoom in, highlight patterns, and tell its own story.

↓ scroll

Every dot is one hour. The x axis is date. The y axis is vehicles per hour. Over six months, this sensor produced a cloud so dense it looks almost solid at rush hour levels.

Zoom into any single week and a rhythm appears. Two tall spikes every weekday, then softer humps on Saturday and Sunday. This is the weekly pulse.

Fold all 4,411 hours onto a single 24 hour axis. The shape that emerges is the single most important pattern in the data: two peaks, at 7 AM and 5 PM.

These two bands are the morning and evening rush. Together they account for roughly 60% of the weekly variance. Any model that can't fit both peaks is already losing.

Now split by weekday and weekend. The weekday curve (blue) keeps the sharp spikes. The weekend curve (orange) is a smooth hill centered on noon. Two populations, same sensor.

Fitting these two curves is what takes a regression model from 22% to 98% explanatory power. Watch the scorecard on the right as we walk through each version.

Model 1 treats hour as a plain number. It can only draw a straight line. A straight line through a two-humped curve misses both humps. Adj R² = 0.22.

Model 2 treats hour as 24 distinct categories. Now it can trace the bimodal shape exactly. Adj R² jumps to 0.85.

Model 3 adds an interaction: weekday and weekend get their own 24 hour profiles. This fixes the last large source of error. Adj R² = 0.95.

Model 5 adds one final predictor: the traffic volume from the previous hour. Autocorrelation in residuals drops from Durbin Watson 0.41 to 1.83. Adj R² = 0.98.

These are the fitted weekday and weekend curves from Model 3. If the model were bad, these would look wrong. They match the raw averages. The model captured the structure.

Time of day and day of week predict nearly everything. Weather matters, but only marginally. If you want smoother traffic, the lever isn't weather mitigation. It's shifting when people drive.

Data: UCI Machine Learning Repository, Metro Interstate Traffic Volume dataset. Westbound I-94, Minneapolis, March 30 to September 30, 2018.
